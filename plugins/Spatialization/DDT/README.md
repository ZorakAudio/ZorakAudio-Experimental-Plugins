# Designed Distance Topology (DDT)

## What it is
DDT is a **distance and room-perception shaper**.

The current source pushes a sound forward or backward by rebalancing direct versus diffuse behavior, stereo coherence, room timing, and air loss. It is about perceived distance, not just volume or top-end rolloff.

---

## Why use it
Use DDT when a sound feels too “flat on the speakers” and needs to feel:

- farther away
- closer
- more room-connected
- more diffuse
- less bone-dry but not necessarily more reverberant

---

## Quick start
1. Start with **Distance**.
2. Set **Room Size** and **Spread** to define the reflection geometry.
3. Use **Air Absorb** to darken the distant field if needed.
4. Adjust **Width** so the diffuse field sits correctly.
5. Use **Monitor** to solo Direct or Diffuse while tuning.

---

## Main controls
### Distance
The main forward/back control. Higher settings move the sound farther away.

### Spread
How clustered or scattered the reflection timing feels.

### Air Absorb
High-frequency loss with distance and time.

### Width
Stereo width of the diffuse/room field.

### Quality
Density / CPU setting from Eco through Extreme.

### Amount
Wet amount.

### Output
Final output trim.

### Monitor
Normal, Direct, Diffuse, or Bypass monitoring.

### Room Size
Scale of the reflection time geometry.

---

## In one sentence
DDT makes a source feel nearer or farther by changing the perceptual ratio of direct sound to room behavior.
